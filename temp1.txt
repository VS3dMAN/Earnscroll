2. No in-app account deletion mechanism
app/settings.tsx lines 232–259 only have a "Sign Out" button. Sign-out clears the Supabase session — it does not delete the user from auth.users or any of your custom tables.
Why this is fatal: Google's Account Deletion policy (in force since May 2024, expanded April 2026) requires apps that allow account creation to provide:

An in-app path to delete the account and the associated data
An externally accessible path (web URL) for users who can't access the app
Completion within 45 days, with a clear explanation of what is/isn't deleted

You have full sign-up flows (email, Google, Apple, phone OTP) writing to Supabase — that's "account creation." Without delete, you will fail this gate. This is checked in the Play Console "App Content" wizard and is also a manual review item.
3. AccessibilityService used to autonomously block apps — current implementation
android/app/src/main/java/com/earnscroll/BlockerService.kt listens for TYPE_WINDOW_STATE_CHANGED, checks the foreground package against a stored blocked-list, and on time-bank exhaustion launches BlockedActivity (full-screen, NoTitleBar.Fullscreen, excludeFromRecents, blocks back button → returns user to home).
Why this is fatal: Google's October 2025 AccessibilityService policy update (enforced January 28, 2026) explicitly prohibits "any use of the Accessibility API that enables an app to autonomously initiate, plan, and execute actions or decisions." Killing apps on a configured rule is exactly that. Reference: Use of Accessibility API and the Oct 2025 enforcement notice.
Critically, the eligible categories for AccessibilityService Google publishes do NOT include "screen time / app blocker / digital wellbeing." They list only true accessibility tools (screen readers, switch access, etc.). Apps in your category that have shipped with this approach (StayFree, AppBlock, Stay Focused) were grandfathered in and are now under tightening enforcement. New submissions in this category face very high rejection rates.
Compounding issue: your accessibility_service_config.xml declares android:description="@string/app_name" — i.e. the description shown on Android's accessibility settings page is just the literal string "EarnScroll: Screen-Time Gym" with no explanation of what the service does or what data it accesses. Play Console reviewers consistently flag this as insufficient disclosure.
Compounding issue 2: the in-app prompt asking the user to enable accessibility (app/_layout.tsx lines 70–95) is a simple Alert.alert("Enable App Blocker", "EarnScroll needs accessibility access to block distracting apps when your time runs out...") with "Later" / "Open Settings" buttons. Google requires a "prominent disclosure" before requesting accessibility — a dismissable alert is not sufficient. Required: a dedicated full-screen explanation that lists the data accessed and use, with explicit affirmative consent (e.g., a checkbox the user must tick before the "Continue" button enables).
What you actually have to do (pick one):

Option A — Pivot off AccessibilityService entirely. Use UsageStatsManager (you already have PACKAGE_USAGE_STATS declared) plus a foreground service of type dataSync or specialUse to poll the foreground app every ~1–2 seconds. When a blocked app is detected, fire a high-priority notification asking the user to switch away — do not autonomously kill the app. This is how ScreenZen ships on the Play Store. Less effective UX-wise, but compliant.
Option B — Submit the AccessibilityService Permission Declaration Form anyway, attach a video showing the feature, write a justification framing it as a digital-wellbeing tool, and accept that approval is uncertain and may take 1–3 weeks of back-and-forth. Even if approved, the disclosure UI must be rebuilt.
Option C — Distribute the autonomous-blocking version off-Play (F-Droid, sideload, your own site) and ship a Play Store version that monitors-and-nudges without blocking. Your FREE_LAUNCH_MODE flag suggests you already think in terms of two builds, so this is structurally easy.
